The product spec is the default artefact of the PM role. Someone asks "what are we building?" and the answer is: there will be a document. It will have a problem statement, success metrics, requirements, and open questions. It will be reviewed, commented on, and — eventually — ignored.

I've written dozens of them. I've also shipped products without them. The honest conclusion: the spec itself is rarely the thing that makes a project succeed.

## What specs are optimised for

Specs are optimised for approval, not for thinking. They're written to be read by stakeholders in a 10-minute review window. That means they're structured to answer anticipated objections, not to surface genuine uncertainty.

The result is a document that looks complete but often conceals the three or four decisions that actually matter. The hard trade-offs — the ones where you genuinely don't know the right answer — get buried in footnotes or deferred to "future scope."

## What to write instead

The most useful thing I've found is a **decision log** maintained throughout the project, not written upfront. For each significant decision:

- What were we trying to optimise for?
- What options did we consider?
- What did we choose, and why?
- What would change our mind?

This isn't a spec. It's a record of reasoning. It's useful during the project because it forces explicit decisions. It's useful after because it makes retrospectives honest.

## The spec still has a role

None of this means stop writing specs. The spec is useful for alignment — for making sure engineering, design, and stakeholders share a mental model before work begins. But it should be the output of good thinking, not a substitute for it.

Write the decision log first. Then write the spec.
